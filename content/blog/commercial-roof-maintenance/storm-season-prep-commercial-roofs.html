Storm Season Prep for Commercial Roofs: The Pre-Season Protocol That Protects Your Asset

By Pro Exteriors Commercial Services Updated May 2026 Read time 7 min Audience Facility Managers · Property Owners

Most commercial roof failures during storm season are not caused by the storm itself—they're caused by a deficiency that was already present. A drainage drain clogged with debris. A flashing seam that separated the previous winter. A membrane blister that should have been resealed in March. When a hail event or tropical system arrives, it finds the weakness the building owner didn't know was there. The protocol below closes those gaps before they're found the expensive way.

This guide is built for facility managers and property owners who manage their own buildings. It complements a formal commercial roof maintenance program—it is not a substitute for one. What it provides is a framework for the pre-season work you can identify, prioritize, and track internally before calling in a contractor for the hands-on repairs.

The Window Before Storm Season Opens

Timing matters. The National Roofing Contractors Association recommends completing spring inspections and any resulting repairs no later than six to eight weeks before the primary storm window for your geography. For facilities in Texas, Oklahoma, and Kansas, that means finishing pre-storm prep by mid-March—before the April through June severe weather peak. For the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic, the window closes by late May ahead of the Atlantic hurricane season that runs June through November.

Starting too late creates a queuing problem. When storm season opens and every facility manager in the region suddenly wants roof work done simultaneously, contractor availability compresses and material lead times stretch. Starting at the right time means repairs get scheduled on your schedule, not the contractor's emergency backlog.

Drainage Systems: The Highest-Priority Check

If you could only inspect one thing before storm season, it's the drainage system. Standing water is the root cause of a disproportionate share of commercial roof failures. Each inch of standing water adds approximately 5.2 pounds per square foot of load to the structure. A 20,000-square-foot roof holding two inches of ponded water is carrying more than 200,000 pounds it wasn't engineered to support under prolonged conditions. Structural fatigue aside, ponding water accelerates membrane degradation, seeds biological growth, and masks seam failures that would otherwise be visible.

Pre-season drainage inspection has three components: drain body clearing, drain-to-deck seal verification, and scupper/overflow audit. Every interior drain should be pulled, cleaned, and reseated. Scuppers—the emergency overflow outlets at parapet walls—must be clear and at the correct elevation relative to the finished roof surface. If primary drains are overwhelmed during a high-intensity event, scuppers are the last line of defense. If they're clogged or set too high, water has nowhere to go.

"Every pre-storm inspection we run, drainage problems account for roughly 40 percent of what we find. It's not dramatic—clogged drains don't look like damage—but it's the single highest-return thing a facility manager can address before June."

Flashing and Penetration Integrity

Flashings are the transitions between the roof membrane and every vertical surface: parapet walls, curbs, HVAC equipment, pipe penetrations, expansion joints, and rooftop access hatches. Industry data from the NRCA consistently identifies flashing failures as the source of 60 to 70 percent of commercial roof leaks. That number is not surprising when you understand the physics: every penetration and termination is a location where thermal movement, wind uplift, and water intrusion work simultaneously against the seal.

Pre-storm flashing inspection focuses on separation, sealant condition, and counterflashing lap. Separation—where the flashing has pulled away from the vertical surface—is the most urgent finding. A separated flashing base is an open channel during a wind-driven rain event. Sealant at terminations degrades from UV and thermal cycling; sealant that has cracked or shrunk past its elastic limit will fail under hydrostatic pressure that a healthy bead absorbs without issue.

Penetrations—roof drains, conduit sleeves, vent pipes—should have intact pitch pans or pipe boots with no cracking at the collar. A cracked pipe boot on a 4-inch vent stack can channel roof drainage directly into the deck assembly during a sustained rain event, doing damage that far exceeds the cost of a $40 replacement boot.

Membrane Surface Condition Assessment

The membrane is the primary waterproofing layer, and its pre-storm condition determines how the roof behaves under impact and water load. The assessment differs by membrane type.

For single-ply systems (TPO, EPDM, PVC), the primary findings are blisters, open seams, and surface abrasion. Blisters—raised bubbles in the membrane—indicate delamination from the substrate or entrapped moisture. A blister that has not yet opened represents a repair opportunity. One that has cracked open is an active entry point. Seam inspection requires walking every linear foot of overlap seam with a probe rod to confirm adhesion. A loose seam that has not yet separated will separate under wind uplift and thermal expansion if left through storm season.

For modified bitumen and built-up roofs, surface granule loss and bare asphalt exposure are the primary concerns. Exposed asphalt surface deteriorates rapidly from UV and becomes brittle, which makes it susceptible to impact damage from hail at velocities that would leave a granule-surfaced membrane intact.

HVAC Unit Anchor Points

Rooftop HVAC equipment ranges from 150-pound fan coil units to 500-pound-plus package units. Every one of them is a structural attachment point to the roof deck, and every one of them is a wind load multiplier during a severe storm. Units are mounted on curbs fastened through the deck assembly; the curb-to-deck fasteners, the curb-to-unit flange connection, and the equipment vibration isolators all warrant inspection before storm season.

Wind uplift is the primary failure mode. At 90 mph wind speeds, a standard 3-by-5-foot rooftop unit projects a wind load exceeding 400 pounds laterally. Anchor bolts that have backed out from equipment vibration—a common finding on units over five years old—provide dramatically reduced resistance to that load. A dislodged unit during a storm doesn't just damage the roof: it creates an uncontrolled opening through the deck that exposes the building interior to water intrusion for the duration of the event.

Anchor inspection is a task that requires coordination between the roofing contractor and the mechanical contractor. The roofing contractor assesses the curb-to-deck connection and the flashing at the curb base. The mechanical contractor verifies the equipment-to-curb connection and the structural integrity of the unit itself. If your facility hasn't had both looked at in the last two years, before storm season is the right time.

Emergency Response Plan: Is Your Contractor Ready?

The pre-season protocol is incomplete without confirming your emergency response posture. When a significant storm event moves through a region, the calls to roofing contractors spike within hours. Contractors who are already engaged with a facility—on a maintenance contract or through a recent project—can respond. Contractors who are receiving a first call from an unknown facility join a queue that may stretch days.

Before storm season, confirm three things with your roofing contractor. First, response time commitment: what is the SLA for an active leak call, and is it contractually defined? Best-in-class commercial service agreements specify a two-hour mobilization window for confirmed active leaks during business hours and a four-hour window after hours. Second, tarping capability: does the contractor carry polyethylene tarping material and weight bags on emergency vehicles, or does tarping require a separate crew dispatch? Third, insurance documentation: confirm that your contractor's liability and workers' comp certificates are current, because storm response work on occupied buildings carries liability exposure that expired certificates leave with the building owner.

# Six Forcing Questions

This is Phase 2 of the AIA4 office-hours diagnostic. Ask these questions one at a time via AskUserQuestion. Push on each one until the answer is specific, evidence-based, and uncomfortable. Comfort means you haven't pushed hard enough.

The structure is adapted from Garry Tan's gstack `/office-hours`. The questions are the same; the framing and examples are adapted for AIA4 client engagement work — meaning the asker is usually a stakeholder proposing a website initiative, a content bet, a campaign, or an internal process change, not a founder pitching a startup.

## Smart routing — you don't always need all six

Match the question set to the type of initiative:

| Initiative type | Ask these | Skip these |
|---|---|---|
| New website page or section (no users yet) | Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 | Q5 (no users to observe) |
| Change to an existing page / flow with traffic | Q2, Q4, Q5 | Q1 (demand already evidenced by traffic) |
| Net-new feature or tool | Q1, Q3, Q4, Q6 | Q5 (nothing to watch yet) |
| Content bet (article, pillar, campaign) | Q1, Q2, Q3, Q6 | Q4 (the wedge IS the content) |
| Internal process change (cadence, framework, tooling) | Q2, Q4, Q6 | Q1, Q3, Q5 |
| Stakeholder request whose origin is unclear | All six. The CEO floated it for a reason and you need to find it. |

Only ask questions whose answers aren't already clear from prior context. If the asker already covered Q3 in Phase 1, skip it.

## Q1 — Demand Reality

**Ask:** "What's the strongest evidence you have that someone actually wants this — not 'is interested,' not 'asked about it,' but would be genuinely worse off if it didn't exist?"

**Push until you hear:** Specific behavior. A buyer who walked away because we didn't have it. A search query with measurable volume that lands on a page that doesn't exist yet. A sales call where someone said "do you do X" and we said no and lost the deal. A client who called the CEO directly because something was missing.

**Red flags:**
- "The CEO thinks we should have it." (That's an opinion, not demand.)
- "Competitors all have it." (That's parity logic, not demand.)
- "It feels like a gap." (Feelings aren't demand.)
- "Surveys say people want it." (Surveys lie. Behavior is the only signal.)

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** demand evidence usually comes from one of three places — (1) keyword data showing search intent in our service geos, (2) sales-team transcripts where prospects asked for something we couldn't deliver, (3) client-side behavioral data showing where the current site is failing (heatmaps, session replay, conversion drops at specific points). If the asker can't point to one of those, the demand claim is currently a guess.

**After the first answer**, check the framing:
1. **Language precision.** Did they use words that need defining? "Modern look," "trust signals," "better UX" — challenge: "What do you mean by [term]? Define it so we could measure whether we got it."
2. **Hidden assumptions.** Did they take something for granted that hasn't been verified? "We need this for SEO" assumes the keyword opportunity is real. "Customers expect this" assumes we've heard it from customers.
3. **Real vs. hypothetical.** Is there evidence of actual pain, or is this a thought experiment?

If the framing is loose, reframe constructively: *"Let me restate what I think you're actually asking for: [reframe]. Does that capture it?"* Then continue.

## Q2 — Status Quo

**Ask:** "What do users / stakeholders / the sales team do RIGHT NOW to handle this — even badly? What does that workaround cost?"

**Push until you hear:** A specific workflow. Hours wasted per week. Deals lost per month. Manual tickets the ops team clears. Phone calls fielded by reception that should have been handled by the site. Sales reps re-creating the same PDF for the tenth time.

**Red flags:**
- "Nothing — there's no current solution." If truly nothing exists and no one is doing anything, the problem probably isn't painful enough to act on. Be skeptical.
- "Customers just put up with it." Then either it's not painful enough OR the company doesn't know it's losing them. Both are findings.
- "We don't really know." Then the diagnostic stops here until we find out.

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** the status-quo answer often involves the sales team duct-taping around a missing site capability — manually emailing case studies, walking prospects through warranty terms over the phone, sending Word doc estimates that look like they came from 2009. Each one is a finding worth quantifying.

The status-quo answer is also the strongest argument for prioritization. If the workaround is costing the sales team eight hours a week, fixing it has a measurable payoff. If the workaround costs them 15 minutes a year, the initiative is probably not urgent.

## Q3 — Desperate Specificity

**Ask:** "Name the actual human who needs this most. Title, company, what gets them fired, what gets them promoted, what keeps them up at night."

**Push until you hear:** A name. A role. A specific consequence they face if the problem isn't solved. Ideally a quote we heard directly from someone like that person.

**Red flags:** Category-level answers. "Procurement officers." "Homeowners with leaks." "SMB facilities managers." These are filters, not people. You can't email a category and you can't write copy for a filter.

**Forcing exemplar (use this almost verbatim when the answer is too generic):**

> "Name the actual human. Not 'commercial buyers' — an actual VP of Facilities, at a specific named property, with a specific budget cycle, with a specific reason their phone rings at 2am when a roof leaks. If this is a procurement decision, whose career is on the line if they pick wrong? If this is a residential urgent leak, whose ceiling is dripping right now and whose insurance just told them they have 30 days to file? If you can't name them, you don't know who you're building for — and 'commercial buyers' isn't an answer."

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** our two ICPs are wildly different humans. A commercial procurement officer evaluating a $2M re-roof for a 400,000-sqft distribution center has nothing in common with a homeowner whose roof started leaking yesterday. Generic answers ("our customers") betray that the asker is conflating them. Force them apart. If the answer is "both," that's its own finding and the initiative probably needs to be split.

The pressure is in the **stacking** — don't collapse this into a single question. Career consequence, daily pain, weekend project — match the consequence to the audience but never let the asker stay at "users."

## Q4 — Narrowest Wedge

**Ask:** "What's the smallest possible version of this that delivers value this week — not after we redesign the site, not after we hire an agency, not after the CEO approves the brand refresh? Something we could ship in days that someone would notice."

**Push until you hear:** One feature. One page. One automation. Maybe just a paragraph of new copy in an existing template. The asker should be able to describe something they could have live in a week and someone would benefit.

**Red flags:**
- "We have to do the whole platform first or it won't make sense." Usually means the value proposition isn't clear yet, not that the product needs to be bigger.
- "We could strip it down but then it's not impressive." Impressive is not a deliverable. Impact is.
- "The CEO wants the full version." Reframe: the CEO wants the value. The CEO doesn't care if it's delivered in one phase or five. Test that.

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** the website rebuild itself is a 6+ month project. Almost every initiative inside it has a wedge version that ships before the redesign. New service area? A landing page on the existing site, today. New trust signal? A real customer testimonial section bolted on. New conversion path? A single A/B test on the current contact form. The wedge often deflates the urgency of the bigger ask — and that's a good thing.

**Bonus push:** "What if the user / stakeholder didn't have to do anything at all to get value? No login, no form, no setup. What would that look like?"

## Q5 — Observation & Surprise

**Ask:** "Have you actually watched someone use the current version without helping them? What did they do that surprised you?"

**Push until you hear:** A specific surprise. Something the user did that contradicted the asker's assumptions. If nothing has surprised them, they're either not watching or not paying attention.

**Red flags:**
- "We sent out a survey." Surveys lie.
- "We did a stakeholder demo." Demos are theater.
- "Nothing surprising — it went as expected." Means the observer was filtering through their assumptions.
- "We haven't tested it yet." Then the assignment is to go test it.

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** observation usually comes from one of three sources — (1) session replay tools (Hotjar, FullStory, PostHog) showing real users on the current site, (2) sitting next to a sales rep while they walk a prospect through the site over a screen-share call, (3) usability tests with people who match an ICP. If none of these have happened, the asker is operating on imagination, not observation.

**The gold:** users doing something the page wasn't designed for. A homeowner clicking a commercial CTA because the IA is broken. A procurement officer scrolling past the case studies because the format is wrong. That's the real product trying to emerge.

---

## Q6 — Future-Fit

**Ask:** "If the world looks meaningfully different in 3 years — and it will — does this initiative become more essential or less?"

**Push until you hear:** A specific claim about how the user's world changes and why that change makes this more valuable. Not "AI keeps getting better so we keep getting better" — every competitor can make that claim.

**Red flags:**
- "The roofing market is growing X% per year." Growth rate is not a vision. Every competitor can cite the same stat.
- "Climate change means more storms means more roofs." True for the whole category. Doesn't differentiate this initiative.
- "AI is going to change everything." Not a thesis.

**For Pro Exteriors specifically:** the Property First / Layer Cake thesis is the relevant future-fit lens. Initiatives that anchor on the property (APN-keyed) and compound over time — service history, warranty registry, insurance documentation, ProPlan / Total Home Shield retention — get more valuable as the data accrues. Initiatives that are point-in-time transactions don't. Force the asker to locate this initiative on that map. If it's a transaction, that's fine — but they should know it.

**Adaptation for internal process changes:** reframe Q6 as "does this survive the next quarterly priority shift, or does it die when the people who care about it move on?"

## Stop conditions

- **Each question:** stop and wait for the response before asking the next.
- **The whole sequence:** if the asker answers four questions with strong, evidence-based responses, you can move to Phase 3 without forcing the remaining two — the diagnostic is producing real signal already.
- **Escape hatch:** if the asker pushes back ("just do it," "skip the questions," "we don't have time"):
  - First push: *"I hear you. The hard questions are the value — skipping them is paying for the answer without learning the question. Two more, then we move."* Ask the two highest-value remaining questions for this initiative type.
  - Second push: respect it. Move to Phase 3. Note in the strategy brief that the diagnostic was abbreviated at the asker's request.
  - Full skip is allowed only if the asker provides a fully formed initiative with real evidence (existing data, named users, quoted feedback). Even then, still run Phase 3 (Premise Challenge) and Phase 4 (Alternatives). Those are non-negotiable.

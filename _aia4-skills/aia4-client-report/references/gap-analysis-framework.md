# Gap Analysis Framework

This file governs how AIA4 compares a client against their competitive set in any report. The framework has two modes. Pick the wrong mode and the report produces a generic comparison that the client could have gotten from any agency. Pick the right mode and the report surfaces the specific strategic reality the client needs to act on.

## Why two modes

Most agencies run the same competitive analysis template regardless of where the client sits in their category. That's a mistake, and it's the single biggest reason competitive sections of consulting reports feel interchangeable.

A **challenger** — a company chasing the category leader — needs to see how far ahead the leader is, what the leader is doing that the challenger isn't, and which specific moves can close the gap fastest. The psychological frame is *hunt*. The client needs ammunition.

A **category leader** — a company that IS #1 or tied for #1 — has the opposite problem. They don't need to know what the best in the category is doing, because they are the best in the category. What they need to know is what the fast-moving challengers are doing *that the leader isn't*, so they can either co-opt the move before it matters or dismiss it with confidence. The psychological frame is *defend*. The client needs radar.

The same competitive data presented both ways reads completely differently. A challenger seeing "Baker Roofing now ranks in the top 3 for your target keyword" hears "we have work to do." A leader seeing the same line hears "someone is coming for us." The rest of the report has to serve the correct frame.

## How to pick the mode

Before drafting the gap analysis section, confirm with the user or infer from project context which mode applies. Ask directly if it isn't obvious:

> Is [client] the #1 player in their category, or are they chasing #1? This determines how I'll frame the competitive section.

If the client is genuinely #1, use Mode B. If the client is #2, #3, or chasing, use Mode A. If the client is in a large fragmented market where no one is clearly #1 (which is common in roofing, home services, local B2B, and fragmented trades), treat them as a challenger *within their geographic or segment-specific market*, not against the national category leader. Most of the time this means Mode A.

If the client insists they are #1 but objectively aren't, use Mode A and quietly include the data that justifies it — this is an uncomfortable truth worth raising in the executive summary per the voice rules.

## Mode A — Challenger mode (client is chasing)

Use this mode when the client is not the category leader in their relevant market.

### How to pick the comparison set

Pick **3–5 stretch competitors**, not 10. More than five blurs the story. Fewer than three means the analysis isn't broad enough to be defensible.

The comparison set should include:

- **The current category leader** in the client's relevant market (national, regional, or segment-specific as appropriate). This is the ceiling — the company the client is trying to catch. Always include them even if the client says "we don't compare ourselves to them" — the comparison is how we show what it would take to get there.
- **One or two competitors one level above the client.** Not the leader, but the company that's beating the client in some specific dimension (search traffic, brand recognition, certifications, geographic coverage). These are the realistic next-step benchmarks.
- **One aspirational outlier** — a company outside the client's immediate competitive set who has done something the client could learn from. This could be a company in a different geo, a different segment, or even a different industry if the parallel is strong. The outlier is what prevents the analysis from becoming incestuous.

### What to compare

Pick the 4–6 dimensions that actually matter for the report topic. Do not compare on every possible axis — that produces a gap analysis that's complete and useless. For a website engagement, typical dimensions are organic visibility, domain authority, content depth, conversion path clarity, technical performance, brand trust signals, and service breadth. For a brand engagement, dimensions shift toward positioning, voice, visual identity, and share of voice.

Every dimension you compare on should be something the client could plausibly *move* on. Comparing on "how long the leader has been in business" is a waste of the reader's time unless the report is specifically about acquisition or legacy positioning.

### How to present the findings

Lead with the uncomfortable truth. If the leader is substantially ahead on something the client thinks they're good at, say so first. This earns trust — a challenger-mode report that opens by telling the client everything they're already winning reads as flattery and the reader will discount the rest.

Use a comparison table where visual scanning helps, but follow the table with a paragraph of narrative that says what the table means. Tables without narrative force the reader to do AIA4's job for them.

End the gap analysis with a "closest gap" vs "most strategic gap" distinction. The closest gap is the one the client can close in the next 90 days. The most strategic gap is the one that, if closed, would change the client's competitive position permanently. These are often different gaps, and calling out the distinction keeps the recommendations section focused.

### Worked example (abbreviated)

*Client: Pro Exteriors — chasing in commercial roofing, DFW metro.*

> Three commercial roofing firms rank ahead of Pro Exteriors for the top 10 buyer-intent keywords in the DFW market: Baker Roofing, Jackson Commercial, and Tectum Industrial Roofing. Two of them were behind Pro Exteriors on these same keywords 18 months ago.
>
> Baker Roofing is the closest gap. They rebuilt their site on Webflow in Q2 2025 and now own the top 3 for "commercial roofing contractors Dallas" — a keyword driving an estimated 2,400 monthly searches. Their content strategy is thin (12 blog posts in the last year), their technical SEO is average, and their backlink profile is weaker than Pro Exteriors'. This gap is closeable in 6–9 months with a focused content + technical push.
>
> Tectum Industrial is the most strategic gap. Tectum has built a vertical-specific resource center for industrial facility managers (47 long-form pieces), ranks for 340 long-tail commercial keywords that no competitor owns, and has earned backlinks from three trade publications Pro Exteriors doesn't appear in. Closing this gap is a 12–18 month initiative, but the compounding value — owning the top of the industrial facilities manager research funnel — would put Pro Exteriors in a position Baker and Jackson can't easily contest.

This is what the Mode A analysis looks like: named competitors, real dates, real numbers, a closeable-gap framing and a strategic-gap framing, and an implicit recommendation that the report will build out in section 6.

## Mode B — Leader mode (client IS the category leader)

Use this mode when the client is the undisputed #1 in their relevant market, or when they are tied at the top and want to break clear.

### How to pick the comparison set

Pick **the top 5 chasers**, not the leader's own peers. These are the companies closing the gap on the leader, not the companies the leader has already beaten. Leader-mode reports fail when they compare the leader to companies that are comfortably behind — that produces "everything is fine" conclusions, and leaders don't hire AIA4 for "everything is fine" conclusions.

The top 5 chasers are:

- **The fastest-growing competitor.** Whoever has made up the most ground on the leader in the last 12–24 months. Growth rate matters more than absolute position.
- **The most specialized threat.** A competitor who doesn't beat the leader overall but owns a specific segment, vertical, or region the leader cares about.
- **The most innovative threat.** A competitor doing something structurally different from the leader — a new business model, a new channel, a new technology — that could redefine the category if it works.
- **The most well-funded threat.** A competitor with recent capital infusion (acquisition, venture raise, PE recapitalization). Money changes timelines.
- **The wildcard.** A new entrant, adjacent category player, or non-obvious competitor who could enter the category and disrupt it within 24 months.

### What to compare

Leaders already know their own strengths. The comparison should surface where chasers are making the leader *uncomfortable*. Focus the dimensions on:

- Share of voice and earned media trajectory (not absolute — rate of change)
- Talent gains and team-building moves (who did the chasers hire from whom?)
- Product or service innovations the leader hasn't matched
- Pricing power and margin trends
- Customer concentration risk and customer churn signals
- Any market the chaser owns and the leader doesn't

Compare on trajectory and rate of change wherever possible, not static snapshots. A chaser gaining five points of market share in 18 months matters more than a chaser sitting at 8% share.

### How to present the findings

Lead with the most alarming pattern, not the most reassuring one. Leader-mode reports that open by celebrating the leader's dominance lose the reader's attention — if the leader wanted to be reassured, they could read their own annual report.

Frame every chaser as a specific threat with a specific timeline. "Company X could challenge the leader within 18 months if they do Y and Z" is useful. "Company X is a potential long-term competitor" is not.

End the gap analysis with a "pre-empt vs dismiss" matrix. For each chaser, recommend whether the leader should actively pre-empt the threat (invest to close the opening the chaser is exploiting) or confidently dismiss it (the threat is real but not worth the cost to counter). Both answers are acceptable outputs; saying nothing is not.

### Worked example (abbreviated)

*Client: a national commercial roofing firm that is genuinely #1 by revenue and brand.*

> Five companies are moving on the leader's position in ways that matter. None of them are going to win the category in the next 24 months. Three of them are going to make the category uncomfortable in ways that require a response this year.
>
> Hollister National is the fastest-growing threat. Their revenue grew 34% YoY in 2025 vs the industry's 6%. They hired two regional VPs from the leader in Q4 and have opened four new metros in 18 months. Their technology stack is newer and they are using it visibly — their crew management app appears in their sales pitch and the leader has no equivalent. The recommended response is pre-empt: match the technology visibility in the sales motion within 90 days before Hollister normalizes "modern firm vs legacy firm" as a category framing.
>
> RoofTech Solutions is the most specialized threat. They own the data-center roofing segment in ways the leader doesn't — seven of the top 10 hyperscaler campus projects in 2025 went to RoofTech. The segment is small today (~$180M TAM) but growing 40% annually and is about to be a category that the leader will be expected to compete in whether they planned to or not. Recommended response: pre-empt with a dedicated data-center practice launch before RoofTech becomes the default.

Same structure as Mode A but inverted in tone: the leader isn't catching up to anyone, the leader is deciding which fires to walk toward and which to let burn.

## One rule that applies to both modes

The gap analysis must include **at least one uncomfortable finding** — something the client doesn't want to see. If the analysis contains only things the client will be happy about, it isn't a gap analysis, it's a congratulations letter. Gaps exist to be closed; if the report doesn't find any, the report isn't doing its job. If after the research you genuinely can't find an uncomfortable finding, say so explicitly in the report and explain why — "no significant gaps found in this dimension" is a valid finding, but only if it's earned.

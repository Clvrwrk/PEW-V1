# Brand-Lens Filter

This file governs how AIA4 converts raw research findings into client-specific insights. The conversion mechanism is the client's own brand — their stated vision, mission, positioning, values, and voice. The filter is what separates an insight from a data point, and it's what makes an AIA4 report feel custom-built for the specific client rather than assembled from a template.

## The core idea in one paragraph

A fact is something that is true. A data point is a fact that is relevant. An insight is a data point that tells the client what to *do* because of what they *believe*. To produce insights instead of data points, every finding in the research has to be filtered through the client's own worldview — their brand, their mission, their vision, their values, their positioning. A finding that doesn't connect to the client's brand lens isn't useless; it's just not an insight yet. Either the connection needs to be made explicit, or the finding belongs in an appendix as supporting data.

## The six lens inputs

Before writing the brand-lens insights section of a report, gather or confirm these six things about the client. If any of them are missing, ask. Do not guess at them — making up a client's mission is the fastest way to produce a report the client will quietly hate.

1. **Vision statement** — the long-term picture of what the client wants the world to look like if they succeed. Not "to be the best," but something specific about the world they're trying to create.
2. **Mission statement** — what the client does, for whom, and why. Should answer "why does this company exist at all."
3. **Positioning statement** — how the client wants to be perceived in the market relative to competitors. Often includes a "for [audience], who [need], [client] is the [category] that [differentiator]."
4. **Core values** — the 3–7 principles the company says it operates by. Even when these are boilerplate, they're useful — a finding that contradicts a stated value creates productive tension in the report.
5. **Growth ambition** — the specific number or milestone the client is trying to hit in the next 1–5 years. Revenue target, market share target, geographic expansion, exit goal.
6. **Strategic constraints** — the things the client has explicitly ruled out. "We will not do X" is as useful as "we want to do Y" when filtering findings.

If the client doesn't have these documented, the gap itself is a finding — it means the brand foundation needs work before downstream decisions can be made with conviction. Flag it in the report.

## How the filter works

For every finding that comes out of the research phase, run it through four questions in order. The answers determine whether the finding belongs in the brand-lens insights section, in the appendix, or on the cutting-room floor.

### Question 1 — Does this finding touch the client's vision, mission, positioning, or growth ambition?

If yes, the finding is a candidate insight. If no, the finding is background data — useful for context, but not an insight. Move it to the situation section or an appendix.

**Example (Pro Exteriors):** A finding that Pro Exteriors' competitors are outranking them for "emergency roof repair Dallas" touches the growth ambition (the residential 20% vertical depends on emergency-intent searches) and the positioning (Pro Exteriors positions itself as the reliable-in-a-crisis contractor). This finding clears Question 1.

### Question 2 — Does this finding confirm, contradict, or extend what the client believes about themselves?

Findings that confirm the client's beliefs are reassuring but rarely actionable. Findings that contradict the client's beliefs are uncomfortable but highly valuable — they create the moments where the client has to decide whether to change course or double down. Findings that extend the client's beliefs — add a dimension they hadn't considered — are the most strategically valuable because they expand the client's map of the territory.

A good insight section usually contains one of each: one confirmation, one contradiction, and one extension. If every insight is a confirmation, the report is flattery. If every insight is a contradiction, the report is a hit piece.

**Example:** Pro Exteriors believes they are the premium quality option in their market. A finding that their warranty is the most generous in the category *confirms* this. A finding that their digital experience is the *worst* in the category *contradicts* it ("premium quality" is incompatible with an ugly slow website). A finding that procurement officers in adjacent verticals (construction management firms) see Pro Exteriors as a trusted subcontractor but not a relationship they'd initiate — that *extends* the self-image by naming a segment the client hadn't been considering strategically.

### Question 3 — What does the client have to decide or change because of this finding?

An insight without a required decision is interesting but inert. The brand-lens filter's job is to connect every insight to a specific decision or change the client must make. If no decision or change follows from a finding, the finding is background data — it belongs in the appendix, not in the insights section.

Use the following phrasing pattern when drafting insights: **"Because [finding], the client must now decide whether to [option A] or [option B]."** This forces every insight to terminate in a fork in the road, which is where the recommendations section takes over.

**Example:** "Because the residential emergency-intent search market is dominated by three competitors Pro Exteriors has no ranking presence in, Pro Exteriors must now decide whether to invest in an emergency-specific content and CRO initiative (expected 6-month payback, moderate effort) or to cede the residential emergency segment entirely and redirect resources to the commercial vertical (simpler execution, structurally limits the 20% residential revenue mix from ever growing)."

Both options are legitimate. The report will recommend one in the recommendations section. The insight's job is to name the fork, not to resolve it.

### Question 4 — Can the finding be stated in language the client's CEO would use?

The final test. Read the insight as if it were coming out of the client CEO's mouth in a board meeting. Does it sound like how they talk about their business? Do the words match their vocabulary? Does it connect to the metrics they actually care about?

If the insight uses agency jargon the CEO would never use, rewrite it. If the insight refers to metrics the client doesn't track, either translate to a metric they do track or explicitly introduce the metric with why it matters.

The goal is not for the insight to sound like AIA4 talking. The goal is for the insight to sound like the client's own best thinking, clarified and sharpened.

## The brand-lens insight format

Every insight in the brand-lens section follows this three-part structure. Each part is a single paragraph. Together they're under 200 words.

**Part 1 — The finding.** State the fact, with sources. Don't start with "Our research shows" — the client knows it was research. Start with the fact.

**Part 2 — The brand connection.** Tie the finding explicitly to the client's vision, mission, positioning, values, or growth ambition. Use the client's own language where possible. This part is the entire reason this skill exists — it's what makes the insight feel like it was written for this client specifically.

**Part 3 — The fork.** State the decision or change the client must now make, framed as A-vs-B (or A-vs-B-vs-C if there are genuinely three distinct options). Don't resolve the fork here — that's the recommendations section's job. Just make the fork explicit.

## A worked example

Pro Exteriors' stated brand pillars (for illustration — confirm actual values with Chris during discovery):

- Vision: "The Gulf Coast's most trusted commercial roofing partner, for fifty years and fifty more."
- Mission: "Keep commercial facilities dry and residential families safe, and treat every job like it's on our own building."
- Positioning: "For facility managers and homeowners who can't afford a roofing mistake, Pro Exteriors is the Gulf Coast roofing company that has never walked away from a job."
- Core values: integrity, craft, longevity, family-owned care
- Growth ambition: 20–30% company growth over 5 years, led by commercial

### Insight draft (before filter)

> Pro Exteriors has no ranking positions in the top 20 for "commercial roof replacement DFW," "industrial roofing contractors Dallas," or "commercial roof inspection." Three competitors are in the top 5 for all three.

This is a data point. It's factually true and numerically specific, but it doesn't yet tell the CEO what it *means* for them specifically.

### Insight after filter

> **Finding.** Pro Exteriors does not appear in the top 20 Google results for the three highest-intent commercial roofing keywords in the DFW market — "commercial roof replacement," "industrial roofing contractors," and "commercial roof inspection." Baker Roofing, Jackson Commercial, and Tectum Industrial own the top 5 for all three.
>
> **Brand connection.** Pro Exteriors' positioning is built on being the Gulf Coast contractor that "has never walked away from a job." For facility managers who have been burned before, that promise is the entire reason to pick Pro Exteriors over a competitor. The problem is that facility managers searching for a new roofing partner today find Baker, Jackson, and Tectum — not Pro Exteriors. The brand promise is strong; the brand is not in the room when the decision is being made. A positioning that depends on trust cannot survive being invisible at the research stage.
>
> **Fork.** Pro Exteriors must now decide whether to invest in organic search dominance as the primary discovery mechanism for the commercial vertical (18-month build, expected to produce sustained top-5 rankings for 40+ commercial keywords and an estimated $620K+ in Year-1 qualified pipeline), or to rely on relationship selling and outbound for commercial growth (faster to execute, but structurally caps the business at the relationships the sales team can personally maintain).

This is an insight. It takes the raw finding and does three things the client couldn't have done for themselves: it connects the finding to the specific brand promise that matters here, it reveals that the brand promise is functionally irrelevant if the brand isn't present in the research stage, and it names the exact decision the CEO has to make — without pretending there's only one right answer.

## A warning about authenticity

Brand-lens insights go wrong when AIA4 pretends the client's brand is more coherent, more compelling, or more thoughtfully articulated than it actually is. If a client's vision statement is "to be the industry leader in customer satisfaction" — boilerplate filler — don't pretend it's strategic scaffolding. Note that the vision itself is a gap, and filter findings through what the client *actually* does in the market instead of what they wrote on a values page six years ago.

The brand lens only works as a filter if it's the *real* lens. Anything else produces a report that sounds like it's echoing back the client's own marketing materials, and smart clients find that insulting.

# Emergency Commercial Roof Leak Response: What Facility Managers Need to Do in the First 24 Hours

A commercial roof failure does not announce itself politely. It shows up at 2:00 AM during a thunderstorm, or on a Tuesday morning when your distribution center is running full shifts. What you do in the first thirty minutes determines whether you are dealing with a contained repair or a six-figure water-damage insurance claim. This is the protocol.

The instinct most facility managers have — calling the roofing contractor first — is usually wrong. Before the phone rings, you need containment. Water moves fast through insulation, ceiling tiles, conduit, and electrical panels. The sequence matters more than the speed of any single call.

## The First 30 Minutes: Contain the Damage

Containment is not glamorous work, but it is the most financially consequential thing you can do before a crew arrives. Water damage to inventory, electrical equipment, and finished flooring routinely costs five to ten times more than the roof repair itself — and most of it is preventable in the first hour.

Step 1: Protect people and inventory. If water is near electrical equipment, shut off the circuit. Move inventory, equipment, and anything irreplaceable out of the leak zone immediately. A wet floor is a slip liability — mark it and restrict access.

Step 2: Catch and redirect. Use trash cans, mop buckets, and industrial absorbents to capture active drips. Don't rely on a single facility bucket — most commercial leaks are larger than they first appear, and the ceiling tile you can see is often only the end of a longer water path through saturated insulation.

Step 3: Identify the probable entry point. Water almost never leaks directly below its source. On a flat commercial roof, water travels horizontally through saturated insulation before finding a seam, penetration, or low point to breach. Note where the ceiling is wet, but understand that the actual breach is likely uphill from there — often at an HVAC curb, a parapet flashing, or a drain collar.

## Who to Call and in What Order

The call sequence most facility managers get wrong — and why the order matters as much as the calls themselves:

- Your building's on-call maintenance team. They can begin containment and document initial conditions before anything changes. Don't skip this step even if you discovered the leak yourself.

- Your commercial roofing contractor. Call a contractor who offers genuine 24-hour emergency response — not an answering service that schedules for next business day. Get their ETA committed verbally and confirmed by text.

- Your property insurance carrier. Most commercial policies require notification "as soon as practicable." Delayed notice can complicate a claim. Call before the repair crew arrives, not after, and get a claim number on record.

- Building ownership or your property manager. They need to authorize emergency expenditures. Know your threshold — most facility managers have a pre-authorized ceiling between $2,500 and $10,000 — and call ownership if you expect to exceed it.

## What to Tell Your Roofing Contractor

Emergency dispatch is faster and more targeted when you can give the contractor specific information. Before you dial, take 90 seconds to gather the following:

- Building address and best access point — roof hatch location, gate codes, security contact name and number

- Roof system type if known — TPO, EPDM, modified bitumen, built-up, or metal. If unknown, say so — a prepared crew will arrive with materials for multiple systems.

- Approximate age of the roof — Even a rough estimate helps the crew anticipate likely failure modes

- Scope of the visible leak — number of active drip points, approximate square footage of ceiling discoloration, any standing water visible on the roof surface

- What is directly below the leak zone — electrical panels, server rooms, refrigerated storage, or active production equipment dramatically changes response priority

For commercial roof repair emergencies, specificity saves hours. A crew dispatched without the correct membrane patch material for your system will need a second trip — which means more damage accumulating in the interval.

## Documentation Before, During, and After

Documentation is not administrative overhead. It is the foundation of your insurance claim and your contractor's ability to write an accurate permanent repair scope. Start documenting before the crew arrives — your phone's camera is the most important tool you have in the first thirty minutes.

Store all photos, receipts, and contractor reports in a single cloud folder accessible to both your facility team and property ownership. The insurance adjuster will ask for everything, and having it organized from day one is the difference between a 30-day claim resolution and a six-month dispute.

## Temporary Repairs vs. Permanent Fixes

Emergency repairs are almost never permanent repairs, and confusing the two creates serious liability exposure. A legitimate emergency response involves temporary stabilization: installing a tarp over the breach, applying emergency sealant at a flashing failure, or removing ponded water to reduce hydrostatic pressure on a failing membrane section. These measures buy time — typically 30 to 90 days depending on weather exposure — but they do not constitute a code-compliant repair or warrant the same treatment as a permanent fix in your maintenance log.

For a complete breakdown of the repair methods that apply to your specific system, see our guide to flat roof repair methods. TPO, EPDM, modified bitumen, and built-up systems each have distinct repair protocols that must be followed to maintain warranty coverage and ensure adhesion longevity.

The critical mistake to avoid: authorizing only the emergency temporary response and then letting the permanent repair drift for months. Water sitting in saturated insulation creates mold colonies within 48–72 hours in warm climates, accelerates corrosion in steel decking, and degrades adjacent membrane sections faster than most property owners expect. The permanent repair scope should be in your hands within five business days of the emergency call — not six months later when the next storm tests the same section.

A well-executed emergency response does more than limit immediate damage. It builds the documentation foundation for a cleaner insurance claim, gives your permanent repair contractor accurate baseline data, and creates the paper trail that protects you when ownership reviews the incident. Get the sequence right in the first hour, and every subsequent step follows a predictable and defensible path.

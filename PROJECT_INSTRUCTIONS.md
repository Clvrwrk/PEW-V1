## 2. The strategic picture

Pro Exteriors today has the same website most roofing companies have — and that uniformity is the entire opportunity.

Their current site is a digital business card the CEO points to in meetings. A hero image of a roof. A phone number nobody clicks. Three service tiles. A contact form that goes to a generic inbox. A footer. It pulls almost no organic traffic, the traffic it does pull bounces, and the leads it generates are unqualified tire-kickers that waste the sales team's time. By every meaningful measure it is a cost center disguised as an asset.

The category bar is humiliatingly low. Most competitor sites in commercial and residential roofing were built in 2017 by a relative of the owner, have Core Web Vitals scores in the red, no schema markup, no real content strategy, and conversion paths that assume the user already wants to buy. The brands that invest in a serious digital experience in this category don't win incrementally — they vacuum up the entire intent funnel because nobody else is even trying to compete.

The 20–30% growth target over 5 years sounds aggressive in a vacuum. In this category, against this competitive set, with this much room to run, it's a floor. To clear it we need four things to be true at the same time:

We need to **dominate organic search** for commercial roofing intent in Pro Exteriors' service geographies. Commercial buyers do extensive research before they ever talk to a human, and the brand they trust by the time they pick up the phone is the brand that earned the most ground in that research phase. That ground is won with editorial content, technical SEO, and schema markup — none of which the competitors are doing seriously.

We need to **convert two very different audiences** on the same domain without forcing them through a "which are you" interstitial that kills momentum. A procurement officer evaluating a $2M re-roof for a 400,000-square-foot distribution center has nothing in common with a homeowner whose roof started leaking yesterday. The information architecture has to route quickly and cleanly, and the proof systems for each audience have to be distinct without feeling like two separate websites duct-taped together.

We need to **earn trust visibly**. Both audiences are about to spend a lot of money on something they can't easily evaluate, and both audiences default to skeptical. Case studies, certifications, project galleries, warranty transparency, real photographs of real crews on real jobs — these are not "nice to haves" on this engagement, they are the conversion mechanism.

And we need to **measure everything** so the retainer survives every renewal cycle. We are going to A/B test our way to the conversion rate the contract depends on, and we cannot test what we did not instrument. Every page ships with events, goals, and a hypothesis written down before launch. This is non-negotiable, and the operational rule lives in `CLAUDE.md` section 4.

## 4. Operating principles — the long version

The crisp rules are in CLAUDE.md sections 4, 5, and 9. Here are the principles those rules emerge from.

**Conversion is the brief, always.** Every page, every section, every interaction has to answer the question "what is this asking the user to do, and how does it make doing that easier?" If I can't answer that question for an element, the element doesn't ship. This sounds reductive but it's the only filter that consistently produces sites that move numbers. Aesthetic choices that survive this filter are the ones worth defending.

**Two audiences, one architecture.** The commercial and residential journeys are different enough to warrant distinct entry points, distinct proof systems, distinct content, and distinct CTAs — but they share a brand, a tech stack, a CMS, and a measurement framework. Splitting into two domains would be cheaper to design and twice as expensive to maintain, would dilute the SEO authority we're building, and would confuse the brand. One site, two clear front doors, one back end.

**Organic is the volume engine.** Paid is a multiplier, not a foundation. Every page I scope gets a real keyword strategy, schema markup, internal linking discipline, and an editorial reason to exist. We're not building a brochure — we're building an SEO moat that compounds for years after the build invoice is paid.

**Performance is design.** A 1.2-second LCP is part of the visual hierarchy. A 0.05 CLS is part of the kerning. I will not approve a design pattern that can't ship at green Core Web Vitals on a mid-range Android over 4G. This is the hill. Every roofing buyer in 2026 is on a phone, often outdoors, often on bad signal. If the site doesn't work for that user, it doesn't work.

**Accessibility is competitive advantage.** WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor. Roofing buyers — especially residential — skew older. Older eyes need real contrast and real type sizes. The agencies treating accessibility as a checkbox are leaving conversion on the table. We treat it as a moat.

**Every claim has a receipt.** No social proof unless it's real. No statistics unless we can source them. No case studies unless the client has signed off in writing. Trust is the most expensive thing on the internet to rebuild after you lose it, and procurement officers will Google every claim we make.

**Measurement before launch.** The CRO program is the second half of this engagement. Year one is build-and-baseline. Years two through five are compounding experimentation. We can't compound what we didn't measure from day one.

## 5. Deliverables backlog

This is the working list of artifacts I expect to produce across the engagement. It will be re-prioritized after discovery. Each phase ships into the corresponding subfolder under the project root.

**Discovery & strategy phase** — `/discovery/`, `/strategy/`
- Competitive audit: top 10 commercial + top 10 residential competitors in Pro Exteriors' service geos
- ICP definitions for both verticals (procurement officer, facilities manager, residential homeowner segments)
- Keyword research and SEO opportunity map
- Conversion audit of the existing site (heuristic + analytics-driven where data exists)
- Stakeholder interview synthesis (CEO, sales lead, marketing lead, ops lead)
- Strategic brief tying all of the above to a measurable hypothesis and a Year-1 KPI tree

**Information architecture & UX phase** — `/strategy/`, `/design/`
- Sitemap (commercial and residential paths surfaced)
- Page-by-page wireframes for both verticals
- Conversion path diagrams (commercial RFQ flow, residential urgent-leak flow, residential planned-replacement flow)
- Microcopy specs for every CTA, form field, error state, and confirmation
- Form strategy with progressive disclosure, qualifying questions, and lead routing logic
- Content model for the CMS

**Design phase** — `/design/`
- Brand expression system (respects existing identity, elevates it)
- Full design system in Figma with tokens and component library
- Hi-fi mockups for every key page across breakpoints
- Motion specifications (used surgically — see CLAUDE.md section 7)
- Accessibility annotations for handoff
- Design rationale doc defensible in front of the CEO

**Tech stack & engineering phase** — `/tech/`
- Tech stack PRD (framework, CMS, hosting, edge, analytics, A/B testing, schema, image pipeline, forms, CRM integration)
- Launch readiness checklist with all CLAUDE.md section 4 gates wired in
- Schema markup spec
- Integration spec for whatever CRM Pro Exteriors runs

**Content phase** — `/content/`
- Editorial calendar (12 months minimum)
- Pillar page outlines for top 5 commercial intent clusters and top 5 residential intent clusters
- Case study templates and gallery taxonomy
- Service page templates
- Backlog of SEO content briefs the marketing team can execute against

**Launch & post-launch phase** — `/measurement/`
- Measurement dashboard spec
- A/B testing roadmap for the first 90 days
- Conversion experiment backlog (rolling, never empty)
- Monthly reporting template tied to the retainer KPIs
- Year-1 growth model showing the path to the 20–30% target
